About

I am a doctoral student at NYU working with Dr. Marjorie Rhodes. Before NYU, I received my B.A. in Psychology and Linguistics (with a minor in Polish Language & Literature—tak, czasami mówię po polsku) from UC Berkeley. I then worked as a pre-doctoral research associate with Dr. Amanda Brandone within the psychology department at Lehigh University.

Research

Categories are a powerful tool that allow us to make broad inferences about the world based on limited evidence. Broadly, my research is interested in 1) how children learn about the kinds of people, animals, and objects in the world, and 2) what role language plays in children's conceptual development. That is, what information do we convey to children both explicitly and implicitly by the language we use to talk about categories, and how do children's intuitions compare to that of adults?

One line of my research examines how children think about variability versus homogeneity in category. Effective category-based induction requires understanding that categories include both fundamental similarities between members (e.g., all snakes don’t have legs) and important variation (e.g., only some snakes are poisonous). Young kids tend to be biased to assume that categories are largely homogeneous, but they also show some sensitivity to factors limiting generalizability, such category domain and property type. But as children get older, they come to appreciate categories as consisting of diverse members. What are the boundaries of this homogeneity bias, and what drives children to recognize categories as diverse?

Another question I am interested in exploring is how the language we use to talk about categories influences the way that children think about them. One specific example is how generic language affects children's thinking about categories. Generic statements not only provide explicit information about categories (e.g., "Boys like sports" directly states that boys like sports), but also may provide implicit information about categories not mentioned (e.g., "Boys like sports" may imply that girls don't like sports). Do children hold this intuition about generic statements, and if so, does it alter how they represent the distribution of properties within categories?
